01 · Problem

The gap between culture and talent system

Stripe's cultural operating principles are vivid and distinctive. Users First, Macro-optimistic, Exothermic — each carries a clear intent. But without translation into observable behaviors, they stay aspirational. Managers calibrating performance, recruiters assessing candidates, and leaders planning succession need a shared behavioral language grounded in both Stripe's identity and validated measurement science.

The gap

Cultural principles existed as narrative, inspiring but unmeasurable. No consistent framework linked Stripe values to observable talent signals.

The opportunity

Map Stripe's principles to Korn Ferry's validated competency architecture — creating a shared, enterprise-grade behavioral language across the talent lifecycle.

02 · Process

From principles to behavioral architecture

The design process moved from cultural analysis through competency mapping to behavioral translation — with a deliberate distinction between what all high performers need and what makes someone distinctly Stripe-aligned.

1
Cultural inventory
Audited Stripe's published operating principles, leadership expectations, and cultural artifacts to extract the underlying behavioral demands of each principle.
2
Competency alignment
Mapped each principle to the most defensible Korn Ferry Leadership Architect competency, prioritizing predictive validity over surface-level label matching.
3
Tier distinction
Separated competencies into enterprise-critical (what Stripe requires) and culture-differentiating (what makes someone feel distinctly Stripe-aligned) — a distinction with direct implications for hiring bar-setting and succession criteria.
4
Behavioral translation
Wrote three observable behavior indicators per competency, specific enough to use in structured interviews and calibration conversations, not generic enough to apply anywhere.
5
Interactive delivery
Packaged as a clickable explorer rather than a static document — reducing cognitive load and enabling managers to navigate by competency, not scroll through a PDF.

04 · Design decisions

Three choices that shaped the work

Every design decision in this framework was a deliberate tradeoff. Between comprehensiveness and usability, between validation rigor and cultural authenticity, between static documentation and interactive utility.

11
Competencies, not all 38
KF's full library has 38 competencies. Narrowing to 11 required judgment about what Stripe actually differentiates on — not just what sounds good.
2
Tiers, not one flat list
The enterprise-critical vs. culture-differentiating distinction is the framework's key insight — it's the difference between table stakes and genuine Stripe alignment.
3
Behaviors per competency
Three observable behaviors each — enough to be useful in an interview, few enough to be memorable in a calibration conversation.
05 · Reflection

What this work demonstrates

"The hardest part of this work isn't the competency mapping, it's the translation. Taking something like 'exothermic' and making it operationally useful without flattening what makes it distinctly Stripe."

This project demonstrates the ability to sit at the intersection of cultural strategy and talent systems, synthesizing external frameworks, internal principles, and behavioral science into something a manager can actually use on a Tuesday afternoon. This shows how learning moves beyond a training function and becomes part of organizational design.
