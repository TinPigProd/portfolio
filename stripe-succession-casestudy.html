01 · Problem

Succession decisions built on subjective language

Traditional succession planning relies heavily on manager narratives. And those narratives can carry bias. Additionally, phrases like "not a team player" or "too ambitious" can skew talent conversations. These describe personality impressions, not observable behaviors. When those impressions drive 9-box placement, the resulting talent pipeline reflects who leaders like, not who is actually ready for promotion.

The gap

Subjective language in feedback creates inconsistent calibration. The same behavior described differently by two managers produces different placements and different development investments.

The opportunity

Anchor succession decisions to observable, competency-mapped behavioral evidence, reducing bias at the input layer rather than trying to correct for it after placement decisions are made.

02 · Process

From observation to placement

The tool was designed to make the transformation from subjective language to behavioral evidence visible — not just as an output, but as an explicit step the leader can see and learn from.

1
Raw input with bias flagging
Leader enters unstructured observations. The tool flags subjective language at the input layer — signaling that transformation is about to occur before output is generated.
2
Behavioral synthesis
Raw observations are mapped to the Stripe Success Model, stripped of personality language and rewritten as observable, competency-anchored behavioral evidence.
3
Dual 9-box placement
Two views of the same data with Traditional (Performance × Potential) and Success Model (The What × The How). Toggle between them to see how the framework shifts placement rationale without changing the underlying behavioral evidence.
4
Language transformation display
A before/after panel shows exactly how subjective phrases became behavioral observations, making the bias reduction logic explicit and teachable for leaders using the tool.

04 · Design decisions

Three choices that shaped the tool

01
Dual 9-box, not one
Leading with the familiar Traditional view earns trust with a conventional HR audience. The Stripe Model view reframes the same data through a culture-specific lens, meeting leaders where they are before asking them to think differently.
02
Visible transformation
Making the before/after language transformation explicit — not just an invisible backend process — turns the tool into a coaching moment. Leaders learn what objective language looks like by seeing the contrast.
03
Two profiles, not one
Jordan and Priya represent meaningfully different talent profiles: high performer vs. high potential. Showing both demonstrates that the tool responds to the input, not a template.
05 · Reflection

What this work demonstrates

"The 9-box isn't the deliverable. The behavioral language underneath it is. Get the language right and the placement follows."

This tool demonstrates systems-level thinking about talent, not just what to build, but why the input layer matters as much as the output. By addressing bias at the observation stage rather than the calibration stage, the tool changes the quality of the conversation, not just the format of the output. That's the difference between a learning tool and a talent system.
